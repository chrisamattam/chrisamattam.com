## Why now, why this way

This wasn't built as an internal efficiency tool — it was treated as the core product of the business, because it was the thing that could actually separate Butter Money from the rest of the market.

<table>
  <tbody>
    <tr>
      <td>Market context</td>
      <td>DSAs — the standard players in this space — ran eligibility the same way we used to: manual, guesswork-based, dependent on individual experience</td>
    </tr>
    <tr>
      <td>The bet</td>
      <td>Owning eligibility through technology and product, not people, was a structural advantage DSAs couldn't easily replicate</td>
    </tr>
    <tr>
      <td>Team</td>
      <td>~12 people total — Founder, Product (me), Chief of Staff, 1 ops/loan officer on the business side; fractional CTO + 2 full-time engineers + 2 interns on tech</td>
    </tr>
    <tr>
      <td>Build split</td>
      <td>Product owned the business logic — identifying and quantifying lender rules (~2 months). Engineering owned system architecture and implementation (~3 months), in constant back-and-forth with product as edge cases surfaced</td>
    </tr>
    <tr>
      <td>Friction</td>
      <td>The project outlived a couple of technical leadership transitions before shipping — a few fractional CTOs came and went before landing on the one who saw it through</td>
    </tr>
  </tbody>
</table>

## What we built

The core decision: encode lender eligibility rules into a rules engine that could evaluate applicants in memory, instead of querying a database for every case.

<table>
  <tbody>
    <tr>
      <td>Engine</td>
      <td><a href="https://github.com/jruizgit/rules">durable_rules</a> (open-source) — built on the Rete algorithm, the same forward-chaining approach used in classic expert systems</td>
    </tr>
    <tr>
      <td>How it works</td>
      <td>All lender eligibility rules are loaded into memory once. Each applicant is evaluated against them directly — no per-query database lookup</td>
    </tr>
    <tr>
      <td>Why it mattered</td>
      <td>Offer computation dropped from minutes to microseconds, because eligibility checks stopped being a search problem and became a memory lookup</td>
    </tr>
    <tr>
      <td>Surfaces powered</td>
      <td>Consumer-facing eligibility calculator, internal ops tooling, channel partner portal</td>
    </tr>
    <tr>
      <td>My role in the build</td>
      <td>Embedded through the full engineering phase — continuously testing rule outputs and engine performance, flagging production-readiness. Iteration continued post-launch as new lender data and edge cases came in</td>
    </tr>
  </tbody>
</table>
